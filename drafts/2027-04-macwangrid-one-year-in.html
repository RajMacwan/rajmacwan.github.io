MacwanGrid: One Year In

A year ago this week I published the first post on MacwanGrid with no real plan beyond "write the kind of thing I wish more senior practitioners wrote." Twelve months and several dozen posts later, the site has taught me more than I've taught it. This is a short accounting of why it exists, what it's given me, and what's next.

Why I started the site

The honest origin is that I was tired of the trade press. Most of what passes for IT and security commentary online is either vendor-adjacent content marketing, breathless coverage of the latest incident, or conference-circuit thought leadership that avoids committing to anything specific. There's less writing from working senior practitioners than you'd expect, and what exists is often locked behind LinkedIn posts that get swallowed by the algorithm in 48 hours.

I wanted a durable place to think out loud. Somewhere I could be wrong in public, take the feedback, and do better the next time. A site I'd still be maintaining in ten years if the work continued to be worth doing.

The format — nine "grids" covering different facets of the work, from threat intel to practitioner craft — came from wanting to write about the whole job, not just the parts that drive clicks. The career I actually have requires me to think about identity architecture, about vendor negotiations, about how to mentor a junior engineer, about what to read on a Sunday afternoon. The site reflects that.

What the writing practice has given me

Three things I didn't anticipate:

Sharper thinking at work. The act of sitting down to explain a decision to a reader who doesn't share my context has made me better at explaining those same decisions to my team, my peers, and my executive stakeholders. Writing is the debug loop for thinking. If I can't articulate it on the page, I don't actually understand it — and that insight has saved me from a handful of confidently-wrong decisions this year.

A reason to read more carefully. I read more now than I did before the site, and I read differently. Every paper, every breach report, every vendor whitepaper is now a potential source for a post, which means I read with a pen in hand instead of scanning. The depth of what I retain has measurably increased. I can tell because I can use more of it in conversation.

A different kind of relationship with peers. The readers who've emailed, pushed back, or quoted posts in their own work have become some of the most substantive professional relationships I have. Writing publicly attracts a self-selected audience of people who also want to think carefully about the work, and those are the people worth knowing.

What readers have taught me

More than I expected. A few themes from a year of reader mail:

  • Practitioners at mid-market companies see the same threats as F500 peers, earlier in the curve, with fewer resources. I need to write for them more directly. (I've been trying since the 2026 year-in-review post, and I'm still not there.)
  • The audience wants technical depth paired with practitioner judgment. Either alone is less valuable than both together.
  • Readers notice hedging. They also notice overconfidence. The sweet spot is specific claims with explicit uncertainty, and it's harder to hit than it sounds.
  • The posts that felt most personally exposing — the MIT reflections, the ten-principles post, the honest AI retrospective — are the ones readers respond to most. That's probably a general truth about writing and one I should internalize faster.
Writing in public is a slower, harder, more useful form of professional development than almost anything else I've tried. It's not for everyone, but I suspect it's for more people than try it.

What's next for the site

Three commitments for year two:

Deeper technical posts. The reader signal for more technical depth has been clear and consistent. Expect more posts that go into the weeds on detection engineering, identity architecture, AI agent security, and operational practices. Less meta-commentary, more working content.

A MacwanGrid newsletter. Monthly, summarizing what I published and what I read that month. Not a repackaging of the posts — a curated reading list and a short set of notes for readers who prefer email to RSS. Launching this summer.

A guest-post series. I want to host two or three posts a year from practitioners whose work I respect and whose perspective differs from mine. The goal isn't to scale the site's voice; it's to deliberately introduce disagreement into it. Readers benefit when they see thoughtful people contradict each other carefully.

What the site won't do: sponsored content, vendor reviews that aren't clearly disclosed, or any shift toward growth-for-its-own-sake. The readership is the right size if it's the right readers. I'd rather have a thousand senior practitioners who read every post than a hundred thousand drive-by clicks.

The takeaway

If you've been reading MacwanGrid for any part of the past year — thank you. The specific act of you making time to read something long-form in an attention economy that rewards the opposite is the entire reason the site exists. I take it seriously, and I'll keep trying to earn it.

If you've been thinking about starting your own site, your own writing practice, your own version of this: do it. Don't wait until you're senior enough, or until you've got enough to say, or until the market isn't saturated. There is no market. There are just readers looking for someone who sounds honest. Be that person. I'll be one of your readers.

On to year two.
