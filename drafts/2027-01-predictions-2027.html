Predictions for 2027: What IT Leaders Should Plan For

Annual prediction posts are usually a vehicle for being interestingly wrong in public. I try to be boringly correct instead. Five predictions for 2027, each with enough specificity that I can grade myself honestly twelve months from now.

1. Enterprise AI splits cleanly into "real value" and "abandoned pilots"

2026 was the year every enterprise signed a contract for something with "AI" in the name. 2027 is the year the CFO asks which of those contracts to renew. I expect a 40-60% cull of active enterprise AI pilots by Q4, with the survivors clustered in a predictable set of use cases: customer support augmentation, code generation for engineering teams, document analysis in legal and compliance, and a handful of narrow domain-specific applications.

What won't survive: "copilot for everyone" deployments without measured productivity delta, autonomous-agent pilots without human-in-the-loop, and any tool whose ROI case relied on "knowledge worker hours saved" without instrumentation to prove it. The vendors will pivot their marketing accordingly. The word "agent" will quietly get redefined to mean "workflow with a language model in it," which is what it should have meant all along.

For IT leaders: if you can't produce a measured productivity or quality metric by Q2, your AI initiative won't survive Q3 budget review. Instrument now.

2. Identity-first security architectures become the default for new builds

By the end of 2027, "identity-first" will be the baseline for new enterprise architecture the way "cloud-first" was in 2018. The pieces are all in place: phishing-resistant MFA is mainstream, CAE is widely deployed, ITDR (identity threat detection and response) has graduated from a vendor category to a table-stakes capability, and mature non-human identity platforms are shipping.

The organizations that will struggle aren't the ones without the tools — it's the ones without the data model. If you can't answer, today, "who has access to this data and why," you will spend 2027 rebuilding that foundation while everyone else moves on to the next layer. This isn't optional anymore.

The question on every architecture review in 2027 will be "what identity does this use, and what happens if it's compromised?" If your team can't answer both parts, they're not ready.

3. Post-quantum crypto stays a 2029-2030 problem, but the inventory work becomes 2027 table-stakes

I don't expect a cryptographically relevant quantum computer to arrive in 2027. I don't expect it in 2028 either. NIST's post-quantum standards are finalized, the algorithms are ready, and browser and TLS library support is accelerating — but the actual migration pressure for enterprises is still several years out.

What will happen in 2027 is that regulators, auditors, and large customers start asking for crypto inventory. "Where are you using RSA and ECC? What are the key sizes? What's your migration plan?" These are not urgent operational questions yet, but if your answer to any of them is "I'm not sure," that's a 2027 project. The inventory work is the unlock — once you know what you have, the migration sequencing is tractable.

For IT leaders: budget a quarter of a FTE in 2027 for crypto inventory. Small spend, large future option value.

4. The post-Broadcom VMware landscape stabilizes, but not back to where it was

Three years after the acquisition, the migration-away-from-VMware conversation shifts from "are we leaving" to "what's the steady state." A real chunk of the market will have landed somewhere — Nutanix, Proxmox, OpenShift Virtualization, hyperscaler-native — and the remaining VMware customers will be the ones for whom the pricing, however painful, is still cheaper than the migration.

What's interesting isn't the migration itself. It's the capability loss some organizations will discover they've absorbed. The ecosystem of mature VMware-specific tooling — backup, DR, network virtualization — is being rebuilt on other platforms, and "rebuilt" usually means "at 80% of the old capability, and you'll miss the missing 20% at 3 AM." Expect a wave of "we migrated and have regrets" posts from thoughtful practitioners in the back half of 2027.

For IT leaders mid-migration: preserve institutional memory of what VMware was doing for you that you might not have noticed. The operational gaps will be boring and specific — a snapshot workflow, a network segmentation pattern, a DR runbook that assumed vMotion. Inventory them before you lose the expertise on your team.

5. AI regulation starts to bite in ways that affect IT operations directly

The EU AI Act is in enforcement. US state-level AI laws are compounding. Sector-specific regulators — financial services, healthcare, education — are publishing AI-specific guidance that carries real penalties. 2027 is the year where "AI governance" stops being a policy team problem and becomes an IT operations problem.

Concretely: expect audit requests for model inventory (what LLM is used where, with what data), model-decision logs (can you reproduce why the system made a decision), and vendor attestations for AI features embedded in SaaS you didn't know had AI features. Your Microsoft, Salesforce, and ServiceNow tenants now ship with AI capabilities by default; your compliance team will want to know which ones are on, who turned them on, and what data they touched.

For IT leaders: if you don't have an AI inventory for your environment by end of Q2, start there. The regulator will ask, the auditor will ask, and the legal team will ask — probably in that order.

The takeaway

None of these predictions are bold. That's the point. The interesting work in 2027 isn't guessing what will happen; it's executing against what's already happening with discipline. If you do the unglamorous inventory work — crypto, AI, identity — and the boring instrumentation work on your AI initiatives, you will be in the top quartile of peer programs without needing to be clever.

I'll grade this post in December. If I'm wrong on three or more, I'll say so. If I'm right on all five, I'll be suspicious of myself — certainty is the tell that you weren't looking hard enough at the downside.
