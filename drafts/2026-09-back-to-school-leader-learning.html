Back to School: What Every IT Leader Should Keep Learning

The most dangerous moment in a technology career isn't when you don't know enough. It's when you're senior enough that nobody is going to make you learn anything new.

I'm 20+ years into this work and I went back to school anyway — MIT Sloan, part-time, in the middle of a VP role. Plenty of peers asked me why. The honest answer is that I could feel myself starting to confuse experience with understanding, and the only reliable antidote I know is to sit in a room where someone smarter asks me questions I can't answer on reflex.

What MIT actually changed

I'll write a fuller reflection on the MIT experience in February, but for back-to-school season the honest version is this: the content matters less than the discipline. I knew the operations research, most of the finance, a lot of the strategy frameworks. What I didn't have was a structured forum that forced me to defend my intuitions with data in front of people who had no incentive to agree with me.

The class that has stuck with me most so far wasn't the one I expected. It was a course on system dynamics — Sterman's descendants, stock-and-flow models, feedback loops. I've been running IT organizations for two decades and I hadn't had the vocabulary to describe why certain dysfunctions are structural rather than personal. I do now. That alone was worth the tuition.

Why continuous learning matters more at the VP level, not less

The pitch is usually framed as "the field moves fast, keep up." That's true but small. The real reason is that the penalty for being wrong scales with your altitude. A junior engineer who misunderstands a system breaks a ticket. A VP who misunderstands a system breaks a budget cycle, a hiring plan, and a year of roadmap. The surface area of my ignorance is worth more than it used to be, which means I have to work harder to close it.

The most expensive thing in a senior career is an old mental model you forgot to update.

The other reason is simpler: the people on my team deserve to work for someone who is still growing. I've worked for leaders who stopped learning in 2012 and were still running the playbook from 2012 in 2022. It's a bad experience, and the talent you want to keep always notices first.

What I actually use — free and affordable

Not everyone needs a graduate program. Most of the learning I've done that mattered cost nothing or close to it. In no particular order, what's earned its seat on my calendar this year:

  • MIT OpenCourseWare. Free. The 6.824 distributed systems lectures alone are better than most paid training. If you want a specific recommendation, Nancy Lynch's distributed algorithms materials and the Sloan strategy courses are where I'd start.
  • Stanford CS149 / CS155 / CS244. Free video lectures, free problem sets. Parallel computing, security, advanced networks. Sit with one for a semester.
  • USENIX conference papers. LISA, SREcon, and Enigma publish nearly everything. One paper a week, on lunch, will change how you think about your own systems in 12 months.
  • The Google SRE books. Still free online. I re-read the postmortem and error-budget chapters once a year; every year I find something I missed.
  • O'Reilly Learning subscription. The one paid resource I'd defend to my CFO. Not because of the books — I own most of those — but because of the conferences and video libraries bundled in.
  • A written reading practice. Not a tool, a habit. I take notes, in prose, on every technical paper or long-form piece I finish. The act of writing the summary is where the learning actually happens. Obsidian, a text file, a paper notebook — the format doesn't matter. The practice does.

Conferences worth the flight

I've become picky about conferences. Most of the big vendor-sponsored ones are trade shows dressed up as learning events. The ones I still attend because they pay back the time:

  • Black Hat / DEF CON — still the high-water mark for original security research, if you skip the vendor floor and focus on the technical tracks.
  • SREcon — the most substantive operations-focused event on the calendar. Small, technical, under-marketed.
  • MIT CIO Symposium — I'm biased now, but the peer conversations are the reason to go, not the speakers.
  • Regional ISAC events — the most valuable intel I get in a given year comes from sector-specific ISAC meetings where people talk honestly about what's hitting them. Nobody writes about these. Go.

The takeaway

Pick one thing, this month. One paper a week, one course a quarter, one conference a year. Put it on the calendar the same way you'd put a board meeting on the calendar, because for your own growth trajectory, it is one.

The senior leaders I respect most all have this in common: they can still be surprised by what they don't know, and they still find that exciting. That's not a personality trait. It's a practice. Start yours this month.
