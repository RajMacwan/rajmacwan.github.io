Summer Reading: The Security & Tech Books I Recommend

Every July I get the same question from junior engineers and new IT leaders: "What should I read?" The honest answer is "not the latest bestseller on cybersecurity." The books that shaped how I actually do the job are older, narrower, and mostly not marketed as career development.

Here are nine I keep returning to. Some are history, some are craft, some are management. None of them are vendor-adjacent. Read one in the hammock, read two, skip the ones that don't sound interesting — but read something that isn't a Slack thread this summer.

The history books

Countdown to Zero Day — Kim Zetter. The definitive account of Stuxnet, and still the best long-form reporting ever written about an offensive cyber operation. What makes it durable isn't the technical detail (which is excellent) but the institutional storytelling — how intelligence agencies, industrial control vendors, and academic researchers circled the same worm without talking to each other. If you ever have to explain to a board why nation-state threat is different from ransomware, give them this book.

The Cuckoo's Egg — Cliff Stoll. Written in 1989 about events from 1986, and every incident responder should read it anyway. Stoll — an astronomer, not a security pro — chases a $0.75 accounting discrepancy into a KGB-linked hacker. The book is a masterclass in curiosity, rigorous notetaking, and working with institutions that don't want to help you. Half of modern IR methodology is in here, invented accidentally.

Ghost in the Wires — Kevin Mitnick. Mitnick's autobiography is the canonical read on social engineering — not because of the tradecraft (though the tradecraft is impressive) but because of how clearly it shows that humans, not systems, are the permanent attack surface. Read it alongside a modern phishing report and you'll realize how little has actually changed.

Kingpin — Kevin Poulsen. The rise and fall of Max Butler and the carding underground. Poulsen is a former black-hat-turned-journalist and it shows in the precision of the reporting. It's the best book I've found on how criminal marketplaces actually organize, which matters because modern ransomware-as-a-service is the direct descendant of the carding era.

The craft and career books

Tribe of Hackers — Marcus Carey & Jennifer Jin. An interview collection across dozens of security practitioners — red team, blue team, CISOs, researchers. The genre is usually shallow; this one isn't. I hand it to junior folks considering whether to go into security, and to mid-career folks trying to figure out what they want their next five years to look like. The honest answers from working practitioners are worth the cover price.

The Phoenix Project — Gene Kim et al. Yes, it's a novel. Yes, it's about DevOps. Read it anyway. I've given more copies of this book to non-technical executives than any other — because it shows, in narrative form, why unplanned work kills organizations, why constraints matter, and why "more people" is almost never the answer. If you manage an operations team, there is a character in this book who is you. That recognition is the point.

Accelerate — Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, Gene Kim. The data-backed companion to The Phoenix Project. Short, dense, empirical. The four DORA metrics (deployment frequency, lead time, MTTR, change failure rate) have become industry standard for a reason, and this is where they come from. If you are building or fixing an engineering org, this book is the closest thing we have to established science.

The reference books that became philosophy

Site Reliability Engineering — Google (Beyer, Jones, Petoff, Murphy). The "SRE book" is free online and everyone claims to have read it. Few actually have, cover to cover. Do it. Read the chapters on error budgets, on postmortems, and on the embedded SRE model at least twice. It's not just operations guidance; it's a theory of how to run a complex system without burning your people out. That theory is portable to any IT organization, not just hyperscalers.

Designing Data-Intensive Applications — Martin Kleppmann. The one technical book I'd put on every IT leader's desk. Kleppmann writes about distributed systems the way Feynman wrote about physics — the abstractions are made concrete, the trade-offs are made explicit, and the history is respected. I don't write distributed systems code anymore, but I make buy/build/architect decisions about systems built on these principles every week. This book is the reason I can ask the right questions of the people who do.

The takeaway

Two rules for technical reading I've landed on after 20 years: old books first, new books second, because the lessons that survived 20 years survived for a reason. And narrative before reference, because a story you remember is worth more than a framework you don't.

Pick one. Start this week. The security industry will still be here in September when you're done.
