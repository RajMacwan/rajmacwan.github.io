Q3 Threat Landscape: The Trends That Surprised Me

Threat-landscape posts are usually a catalog of what you already knew. This one isn't. Q3 2026 had three developments I genuinely didn't expect, and one that played out exactly as forecast. I'm going to focus on the surprises, because those are where the learning is.

My May post called identity the new attack plane and predicted continued ransomware brand reshuffling. Both right, neither interesting at this point. Let's talk about what changed.

Surprise 1: Indirect prompt injection went from theoretical to operational

I flagged this as a "beginning of a category" issue in May. I did not expect it to become a working, repeatable TTP within five months. We are now tracking at least four distinct incidents this quarter where attackers embedded prompt-injection payloads into documents, emails, or web content that were then ingested by enterprise AI agents with tool access.

The most instructive case, publicly discussed at a summer conference: a customer-support AI agent with access to a ticketing API was fed a support ticket containing instructions to enumerate and email out other tickets containing PII. The agent did what agents do — it followed the instructions in its context window — and the exfiltration was complete before the human operator noticed.

If you gave an LLM agent a tool, you gave the attacker a tool. The only question is how the attacker reaches the context window.

What this means for Q4: if you have deployed LLM-backed agents with any tool use against any untrusted input, you need a threat model specifically for indirect prompt injection. Not someday. This quarter. The mitigations exist — input segregation, tool-call approval gates, capability restriction — but none of them are on by default in vendor platforms.

Surprise 2: The ransomware pivot to pure data extortion accelerated faster than I expected

I thought the encryption-free extortion model would grow this year. I didn't think it would overtake traditional ransomware so fast. Based on public data and what I'm hearing from peers, more than half of Q3 extortion incidents involved no encryption at all. Just exfiltration, public leak-site threat, payment demand.

Two reasons I missed the pace:

  • Backup and recovery actually got better. Immutable backups, air-gapped copies, and faster restore tooling made encryption less of a leverage point. The economics tipped.
  • Cyber insurance adjusted. Insurers are now more willing to cover data-leak settlements than they are encryption-recovery payouts. The industry unintentionally created a market signal that "steal, threaten, settle" is the lower-friction path. Incentives do what incentives do.

For Q4 defensive planning: your data-loss prevention program — which has been the unloved cousin of endpoint security for years — is now the front line. If you can detect 10GB leaving your environment to an unusual destination at 3 AM, you have a fighting chance. If you can't, you will find out about the breach when it shows up on a leak site.

Surprise 3: Nation-state activity moved downmarket

I expected continued APT targeting of defense, critical infrastructure, and tier-one financial services. What surprised me was the volume of nation-state-linked activity this quarter against mid-market companies — manufacturing firms in the $200M–$1B range, regional healthcare networks, mid-sized logistics providers. Not because they're inherently interesting targets, but because they sit inside supply chains that are interesting targets.

We've seen this pattern before — the "island-hopping" concept is a decade old — but the scale is new. A mid-market manufacturer whose CrowdStrike tenant I helped review this quarter had persistent access going back eight months from an actor that had no plausible interest in them except as a stepping stone to a larger customer.

If you are a mid-market IT leader reading this: you are a target. Not because of what you have, but because of who you do business with. Start thinking like a primary target because, from the attacker's perspective, you effectively are one.

The non-surprise: supply chain stayed supply chain

The one thing that played out exactly as expected was continued compromise of the software supply chain. Another npm package incident in July, another GitHub Actions ecosystem event in August, another widely-used build tool with a compromised maintainer in September. If you haven't pinned your dependencies to commit SHAs, rotated your CI secrets, and moved to OIDC federation for cloud access, you are not behind — you are actively exposed. The industry has had four years of warning signs. There is no excuse left.

What's coming for Q4

Three things to plan for now, before the holiday code freeze hardens everyone's posture into whatever state they're in:

  • Holiday-season identity attacks. Black Friday through New Year is always the season for credential stuffing and session hijacking at scale. With the current tooling maturity, I expect the worst volume we've seen.
  • AI-agent exploitation as a research target. The conference season has energized the offensive-research community on this. Expect public tooling and proof-of-concept attacks against major vendor platforms in Q4. Budget a week of your team's time to review exposure.
  • End-of-fiscal-year phishing. The "urgent wire transfer before year-end" pattern is perennial and will be better-crafted this year because of LLM tooling. Rehearse the finance-team response playbook before Thanksgiving, not after.

The takeaway

If you read nothing else from this post: threat-model your AI agents this quarter. Every other trend in this post was a continuation of something established. That one is a category that crossed from theoretical to operational in 90 days, and most programs I've reviewed this quarter have no coverage for it. Close that gap before Q1. It's the kind of thing that will show up in a 2027 post-mortem if it doesn't.
