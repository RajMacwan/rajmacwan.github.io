Evals Are the Unit Tests Your LLM Feature Doesn't Have

You would never ship a payment system with no tests. Yet most teams ship LLM features with no evals at all, then "improve" them by changing the prompt and eyeballing a few outputs. That is not engineering. It is vibes with a deploy button. Evals are the unit tests of an LLM system, and until you have them, you are not iterating — you are guessing and hoping the guesses trend upward.

Why "it looks better" is a trap

Without evals, every prompt change is evaluated by spot-checking a handful of outputs. This feels reasonable and is deeply misleading. You fix the one bad case you noticed, ship it, and silently break three cases you did not look at. Because generation is non-deterministic and inputs vary widely, small samples tell you almost nothing about aggregate behavior. Teams in this mode oscillate: every change fixes something visible and breaks something invisible, and quality wanders sideways for months while everyone feels busy.

What an eval actually is

An eval is a set of representative inputs paired with a way to judge whether the output was good. That is the whole idea. It ranges from simple to sophisticated, and you should start simple:

  • Exact or structural checks. For anything with a right answer — extraction, classification, valid JSON, a required field — you can assert programmatically. These are cheap and should exist first. They catch regressions instantly.
  • Reference-based scoring. For tasks with a known-good answer, compare the output against it. Not always applicable, but powerful where it is.
  • LLM-as-judge. For open-ended quality — tone, helpfulness, faithfulness to sources — a separate model call scores the output against a rubric. Imperfect, but consistent and scalable, and far better than a human glancing at five examples.
  • Human review on a sample. Still the gold standard for the subtle cases. The trick is to spend it where automated evals cannot reach, not on everything.

The smallest eval set that changes everything

You do not need a thousand cases to start. You need twenty to fifty that represent the real distribution of inputs — including the weird ones, the adversarial ones, and the failure cases you have already hit in production. That small set, run automatically on every prompt or model change, converts "I think this is better" into "this moved the score from 0.71 to 0.78 and regressed nothing." The first time an eval catches a change that improved your spot-check but tanked the aggregate, you will never ship without one again.

A prompt change with no eval is a code change with no test: it might be an improvement, it might be a regression, and you have chosen not to know which.

Evals are not the glamorous part of building with LLMs. Nobody demos their eval harness at a conference. But they are the difference between a feature that improves on a measurable curve and one that drifts on the strength of whoever last touched the prompt. Build the small honest eval set first. Everything downstream — prompt tuning, model selection, context work — only becomes real engineering once you can measure it.
