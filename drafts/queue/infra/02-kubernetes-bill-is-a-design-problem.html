Your Kubernetes Bill Is a Design Problem, Not a Discount Problem

When the Kubernetes bill spikes, the first instinct is financial: buy reserved capacity, chase spot discounts, negotiate committed-use rates. Those help, at the margin. But they are treating a design problem as a procurement problem. The largest source of cloud overspend on Kubernetes is architectural — capacity wasted by default because of how workloads were specified — and no discount rescues a design that throws away half the nodes it pays for.

Where the money actually goes

Three architectural patterns drive most Kubernetes waste, and none of them are fixed by a better rate card.

Requests set by guesswork. Every pod declares how much CPU and memory it requests, and the scheduler reserves that whether or not the pod uses it. Teams, afraid of throttling, pad requests generously — ask for four times what the workload needs "to be safe." The scheduler dutifully reserves all of it, your nodes fill up on paper while running near-idle in reality, and you pay for capacity nothing is using. This single pattern, repeated across hundreds of workloads, is often the biggest line item.

Poor bin-packing. Kubernetes places pods onto nodes like packing boxes into trucks. Mismatched request shapes, aggressive anti-affinity rules, and oversized nodes leave every node partly empty. You are paying for the whole truck and shipping it half full, at scale, continuously.

Idle and forgotten capacity. Non-production clusters running full-size overnight and on weekends. Old deployments nobody scaled down. Autoscalers configured to scale up eagerly and scale down timidly, so the fleet ratchets larger and never shrinks. Capacity is easy to add and, culturally, nobody's job to remove.

The design fixes that actually move the bill

  • Right-size requests from real usage. Measure actual consumption and set requests to reflect it, with headroom based on observed peaks, not fear. This is the highest-leverage change and it is free.
  • Let workloads bin-pack. Revisit node sizes and relax anti-affinity rules that are not earning their cost in resilience. Denser packing directly reduces node count.
  • Make scale-down as aggressive as scale-up, and turn off non-prod when nobody is using it. Idle capacity is pure waste and the easiest to reclaim.
  • Put cost on a dashboard the engineers who create it can see. Waste persists when it is invisible to the people making the design decisions that cause it.
Reserved instances discount the capacity you buy. They do nothing about the capacity you waste. The first is a finance lever; the second is an architecture lever — and the architecture lever is far bigger.

Buy the discounts too — there is no reason to leave committed-use savings on the table. But do it after you have right-sized, not instead. Locking in a three-year commitment on a fleet that is twice as large as it needs to be just guarantees you overpay for longer. Fix the design first; then buy the discount on the smaller, honest bill.
