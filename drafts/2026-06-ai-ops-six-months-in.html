Six Months of Production AI in IT Ops: What Worked

Six months ago I greenlit a handful of LLM-backed workflows into production IT ops. Not a pilot, not a lab — actual tickets, actual changes, actual on-call. I promised myself I would write an honest retrospective at the half-year mark, whatever the results were. This is that post.

The short version: the wins are real but smaller than the vendor deck claimed, the failures are specific and instructive, and the thing I was most skeptical of — fully autonomous agents — is the thing I'm still most skeptical of.

What I deployed, and what it actually does

Three workstreams, in order of impact:

  • Tier-1 ticket triage and draft response. Claude (via API, with retrieval over our knowledge base) reads inbound tickets, classifies them, drafts a first response, and suggests a routing queue. A human always ships the response. Zero autonomous actions on the user-facing side.
  • Runbook assist for on-call. A Slack-native agent that, when pinged during an incident, surfaces the relevant runbook section, recent similar incidents, and a summary of the last 30 minutes of alert telemetry. It doesn't run commands. It just reads and summarizes.
  • Change request summarization and risk flagging. Before a CAB meeting, an LLM pass reads every proposed change, summarizes the blast radius, and flags changes that touch systems with overlapping dependencies.

All three use the same pattern: read and summarize, don't write and execute. That choice is load-bearing.

What worked

The triage workflow was the clearest win. Median time-to-first-response dropped 38% and routing accuracy — measured by tickets that didn't need to be re-queued — improved 22%. The draft responses aren't shipping verbatim, but they get the analyst 70% of the way there on common categories (password resets, access requests, VPN hiccups, printer exorcisms). My team's quality-of-life feedback has been stronger than any metric I could have picked.

Runbook assist saved two outages this quarter, measurably. In both cases an on-call engineer got to the right runbook section in under 60 seconds of asking, where historical MTTR to "I know what playbook to run" was closer to 5-8 minutes. That's not revolutionary, but at 2 AM it's the difference between a contained incident and a bridge call.

Change summarization is the quietest win and maybe the most durable. CAB meetings that used to run 90 minutes now run 45. Not because the AI is making decisions — it isn't — but because everyone walks in pre-briefed.

What failed

Fully autonomous anything. I tried two: an auto-remediation agent for a narrow class of disk-space alerts, and an auto-closing agent for "ticket appears resolved" patterns. Both got pulled.

The disk-space agent cleared a log directory on a system where those logs were the only record of a compliance event. Technically it followed the runbook. The runbook was wrong. A human would have flagged it; the agent didn't.

The auto-closer was worse — it closed a ticket that a user had deliberately left open as a reminder, because the language patterns matched "resolved." The user's trust in the system took six weeks to recover. The lesson isn't "agents are bad." The lesson is that the value of a human in the loop is not the action, it's the sanity check on the premise.

I also killed a "Slack question answerer" that was supposed to deflect routine questions away from my team. It hallucinated a procurement policy that didn't exist, and someone nearly put in a PO based on it. RAG over your actual documents helps, but it doesn't eliminate the failure mode — it just makes it more convincing when it happens.

The unglamorous lessons

A few things I wish someone had told me in December:

  • Evals are the job. I spent maybe 15% of the effort on prompts and model selection and 60% on evaluation harnesses. That ratio felt wrong for months. It's correct.
  • The ROI is in the p90, not the median. The AI doesn't make your best engineer faster. It makes your worst ticket — the 90th-percentile ugly one — survivable. Measure there.
  • Costs are real but manageable. We spend about $4,200/month across all three workflows on API calls. That's roughly 3% of a single L3 engineer's loaded cost. The economics work, but only because we're disciplined about what gets routed to the model.
  • Model choice matters less than people claim. I've swapped between Claude, GPT, and a self-hosted Llama variant for non-sensitive workloads. The workflow design, the retrieval quality, and the evaluation loop matter 10x more than which model is marginally ahead this month.
  • The security review is not optional. Prompt injection via ticket content is a real threat vector. We sanitize, we sandbox, we never let the model call tools on behalf of a user without explicit confirmation. Treat every LLM endpoint like an exposed API, because it is.

The takeaway

If you're a peer considering this path: start with read-and-summarize workflows, invest disproportionately in evals, and be deeply suspicious of any pitch that uses the word "autonomous." The gains are real. They look like 30-40% improvements on specific, well-scoped workflows — not the 10x the vendor decks promise, but more than enough to justify the program.

Six months in, I'm not a convert and I'm not a skeptic. I'm a practitioner, and the tool is earning its seat on the team. That's the highest compliment I know how to pay a piece of technology.
