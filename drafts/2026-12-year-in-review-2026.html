2026 in Review: What MacwanGrid Got Right and Wrong

I started MacwanGrid in April 2026 with one rule for myself: at the end of every year, I would publish an honest audit of what I got right, what I got wrong, and what I learned from readers who told me so. This is the first one.

Writing in public is useful precisely because it makes your errors permanent and searchable. If I'm going to ask readers for their attention, they deserve to see me grade my own homework.

What I called correctly

Three calls I'm willing to take credit for:

Identity as the dominant attack plane. The May post laid this out and the rest of 2026 vindicated it. The Okta token-reuse incident, the IdP replay incident in May, and the continued rise of AiTM phishing kits all slotted into the thesis. This wasn't a bold call — it was table stakes for anyone reading the same telemetry — but I said it clearly and often, and I'd say it again.

Indirect prompt injection graduating from theory to TTP. I flagged this as the "beginning of a category" in May. By October I was tracking operational incidents. That was faster than I expected, but the direction was right.

The ransomware pivot to data-extortion-only. Called it in May, underestimated the pace in October. Right direction, wrong velocity. Half credit.

What I got wrong

This is the more interesting section. Three things I said publicly that didn't age well:

I under-weighted the mid-market nation-state targeting trend. My Q2 and early Q3 coverage focused on the big, marquee incidents. I didn't see the island-hopping pattern scaling down into mid-market companies as fast as it did. A reader from a $400M manufacturing firm wrote a thoughtful email in August telling me his environment looked exactly like the intrusion patterns I was describing at F500 scale. He was right. I adjusted by October, but I was late.

I was too optimistic about cyber insurance as a forcing function. I wrote in November that insurance underwriters were "extremely specific" about required controls in a way that would drive industry maturity. That's partly true, but the signal has been muddier than I made it sound. Renewals are still coming in with wildly inconsistent control requirements across carriers, and a number of clients have told me the market has softened on enforcement this year. I oversold how disciplined the insurance market would be.

The "six months of production AI" post claimed change summarization as a durable win. It's held up for me, but several readers pushed back — reasonably — that their CAB process didn't improve at all when they added LLM summaries, because the bottleneck was never the information, it was the politics. That's a fair critique. The tool only helps where the bottleneck was actually informational. I should have named that constraint more clearly.

The most valuable reader feedback I got all year was not "you were wrong." It was "you were right but for different reasons than you said." Those notes made me a better writer.

The pattern is clear: readers want retrospectives and practitioner lists more than forecasts. That's useful data for 2027 planning.

Reader feedback themes

The most common reader emails, roughly in order of volume:

  • "Please go deeper on the technical detail." Fair. I'll do more of this in 2027 without losing the general reader.
  • "What does this look like for mid-market / small team / non-F500?" Very fair. I'm working in enterprise but half my readers aren't, and I need to translate better.
  • "More on the MIT experience." February post is coming — reader demand earned it a dedicated slot.
  • "Less hedging." I take this one seriously. I hedge because I've been wrong before, but hedging past the point of usefulness is its own form of dishonesty. I'll aim for fewer qualifiers in 2027.

MIT progress

Year one is nearly done. Grades are fine, which is the least interesting thing about it. What's actually happened: I've rebuilt my mental models on system dynamics, on capital allocation under uncertainty, and on organizational design. I've also rediscovered how much I dislike group projects, which is a lesson I apparently had to learn twice. The cohort has been the unexpected gift — I'm now two text messages away from peers running IT programs at scales and in industries I had no exposure to before. That network alone has paid back the time cost.

I'll write the longer reflection in February. For now: the program is doing what I hoped it would do, which is make me uncomfortable on schedule.

What's next for the site in 2027

Three commitments for the coming year:

  • Mid-market translation. More posts explicitly targeted at the IT leader running a team of 5-15 rather than 50-150.
  • Deeper technical posts on the AIGrid track. Eval design, agent threat modeling, prompt-injection defenses. Real, working content, not meta-commentary.
  • Less hedging, same honesty. A stronger thesis per post. More willingness to be wrong in public so I can learn faster.

The takeaway

To the readers who've emailed, pushed back, forwarded posts to colleagues, or simply kept showing up in the analytics: thank you. Writing in public has been the most disciplined form of learning I've ever found, and the only reason it works is that you're reading carefully enough to tell me when I'm wrong.

See you in 2027. The 2027 predictions post goes up January 1st, and on December 31st of next year I'll grade that one too.
