One Year of MIT: What the Program Actually Taught Me

I went back to school as a VP, with twenty years of operating experience, mostly to find out what I didn't know that I didn't know. A year in, the honest answer is: more than I was comfortable admitting, and less than my imposter syndrome warned me about.

This is a longer post than I usually write because readers asked for one specifically, and because I owe the experience a careful accounting rather than a highlight reel. What follows is what actually changed my thinking, what turned out to be less useful than I expected, and where the practitioner-scholar tension sharpened me in ways I didn't see coming.

The frameworks that stuck

Not everything I studied is going to outlive the semester. These did.

System dynamics. I mentioned this in September and I'll expand now. Stock-and-flow modeling, feedback loops, and accumulation reframed how I think about organizational dysfunction. Example: my team had a quality problem I'd attributed to individual performance for years. Modeling it as a system — incoming work rate, review capacity, backlog as a stock, and the second-order effect of backlog on morale — made it obvious the problem was structural. I had been applying the wrong intervention, consistently, for years. I corrected it inside of a month after the course. That one insight paid for the tuition.

Real options theory for capital allocation. Before the program, I thought about technology investments as net-present-value calculations. I now think about them as portfolios of real options — the right-but-not-obligation to scale something up or abandon it. This matters enormously for AI investments, platform migrations, and any decision under uncertainty. The vocabulary from option pricing turns out to be weirdly applicable to IT architecture decisions, and it gives me a cleaner way to defend staged investments to finance.

Organizational design as a diagnostic tool. I thought I understood organizational design. I didn't. Nadler-Tushman's congruence model, Galbraith's star model, and the work on ambidextrous organizations have given me specific frameworks for naming what's wrong when a team is struggling — whether it's strategy-structure mismatch, reward misalignment, or an exploration-versus-exploitation imbalance. I used to diagnose these by gut. Now I diagnose them by checklist and my gut agrees more often than it used to.

Negotiation as a discipline, not a trait. I came in thinking I was a decent negotiator. I was wrong in a specific way: I was a decent relational negotiator and a poor distributive one. The MIT negotiation curriculum, especially the preparation protocols, made me measurably better in vendor conversations this year. Tangibly measurable: one vendor renewal saved $340K in 2026 that I credit entirely to preparation work I wouldn't have done before.

The frameworks that didn't stick (or stuck weirdly)

Not every course landed, and I think it's dishonest to pretend otherwise.

Some of the strategic management content felt like vocabulary I already had, dressed up in different language. Porter's five forces, Blue Ocean, the resource-based view of the firm — I'd internalized this stuff from two decades of practice and books. The courses added rigor but not much insight. That's probably appropriate for students earlier in their careers; it was modest ROI for me.

Some of the finance content was well-taught and I still won't use most of it. I'm not going to build valuation models for acquisition targets; we have a finance team for that. What I needed from finance was the literacy to read their models critically, and the program delivered that in about 20% of the finance coursework. The other 80% was professional depth I won't use directly.

This is a lesson for future students in my position: the value of the program isn't uniform across the curriculum, and pretending it is distorts the actual return.

The practitioner-scholar tension

This is the section I found hardest to write.

There's a real tension between operating experience and academic rigor, and both directions have failure modes. The practitioner failure mode is "I've seen this before, I don't need the theory." The scholar failure mode is "the model is elegant, the fact that it contradicts your experience is an inconvenience." The best conversations I had in the program were with professors who took my experience seriously as evidence — and the worst were with faculty who treated two decades of operating scar tissue as noise in the data.

The thing I had to learn, and it took months: my experience is evidence, but it's also biased, non-random, and based on a sample size of one career. The theory is abstract, but when it's good, it survives precisely because it's been tested against thousands of careers, not one.

When those two sources of knowledge agreed, I gained confidence. When they disagreed, it was almost always a signal that I needed to look harder — sometimes the theory was missing context, sometimes my experience had baked in assumptions that didn't generalize. I got better at telling the difference over the year. I'm not done.

The biggest surprises

Three things I didn't expect:

How much the cohort mattered. I was prepared for the curriculum to be the value. The cohort has been at least half of it. Peers running IT, operations, and security programs at scales and in industries I had no first-hand exposure to — manufacturing, healthcare, defense, international banking. A text message to any of them gets me a 30-minute conversation and a second opinion shaped by an entirely different operating context. That network is the durable asset.

How much I had to unlearn, not learn. I came expecting the program to add new knowledge. A surprising fraction of the work was subtraction — identifying the patterns I'd generalized from my specific career and demonstrating, usually painfully, where those patterns didn't travel. That's uncomfortable in a way I wasn't prepared for. It's also the most valuable part.

How much I'd enjoy the writing. The academic writing demands are higher than I expected, and the discipline of defending a claim to a reader who has no reason to agree with you has sharpened my work output enormously. Readers of this blog are getting a tighter version of my thinking because of the papers I've had to write this year. There's a direct line.

Would I do it again?

Yes, with two modifications I'd recommend to peers considering a similar path.

First: don't do this in the first three years of a new job. The cognitive load of a major role transition plus a rigorous academic program is brutal, and both suffer. I'd been in my current role for years before I started, and that stability was load-bearing.

Second: be explicit with your employer about the time commitment. I underestimated the hours by roughly 30%, and the honesty gap had to be closed mid-year. Peers who negotiated realistic time allocation up front had much better first-year experiences.

The takeaway

If you're a senior practitioner considering a late-career academic program, the question I'd ask yourself is not "will I learn something new?" — you will, but so what. The question is "am I willing to have my existing mental models challenged in ways that will be uncomfortable and sometimes embarrassing?" If the answer is yes, go. If the answer is "I want credentials and networking without the discomfort," save the tuition and join more industry boards.

A year in, I'm still the same person with the same operating instincts. But those instincts are calibrated better than they were, and I know more precisely where to trust them and where to second-guess. That's all I came for. It was enough.
