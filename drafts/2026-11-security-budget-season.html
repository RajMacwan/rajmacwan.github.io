Budget Season: Making the Case for Security in 2027

The worst thing a security leader can bring to a budget conversation is a heatmap. The second worst thing is a number of "breaches this cost the industry last year." If those are the tools in your kit, you are going to have a rough November.

I've sat on both sides of this table — the IT leader asking, and the executive reviewing peers' asks. What follows is what actually works in front of a CFO who has read the same Gartner articles you have, has been pitched by the same vendors, and has a finite pool of capital to allocate across marketing, product, and you.

ROI arguments that work

Three framings that hold up under CFO-grade scrutiny:

1. Cost avoidance tied to a specific, modeled scenario. Not "breaches cost $4.8M on average." That number is meaningless to your business. Instead: "Our Salesforce tenant contains 4.2M customer records. A breach of that tenant under our current insurance policy, our current regulatory exposure (GDPR + state laws), and industry-benchmark notification costs would run $18M–$34M. This $700K control reduces the probability of that specific scenario by approximately 60% based on [named case studies]." That's a conversation a CFO can actually engage with.

2. Insurance premium and retention impact. Cyber insurance underwriters are now extremely specific about what controls they expect. Your broker can give you, in writing, the premium and deductible delta for specific control changes. "MFA on all admin accounts reduces our retention by $500K and our premium by $180K" is a number that lands in a finance review, because finance already trusts insurance math.

3. Engineering productivity, measured honestly. If a security tool actually makes engineering faster — SSO that reduces friction, a secret manager that removes manual rotation — quantify the hours. Don't inflate. A modest, defensible productivity claim beats a huge undefendable one every time.

ROI arguments that don't work

Three framings I'd retire from your deck:

  • "If we get breached…" without a model. The CFO's answer is "we might not." You need to move the conversation from possibility to probability, and from probability to expected loss, or you're telling ghost stories.
  • Compliance-as-justification alone. "We need this for SOC 2" works for the first pass. By the third ask of the cycle, the CFO is going to ask which controls map to which requirements, and whether a cheaper control would satisfy the same clause. Have that answer ready or don't lead with compliance.
  • "Our peers spend 8% of IT budget on security." Benchmarking is a weak argument. Your peers might be overspending. Your business might be in a different risk class. Use it as context, never as primary justification.

What to cut honestly, what to defend

Every budget season I go through my own program with a question: if I had to cut 15%, what would I cut? The exercise is useful even in years where I won't have to. Here's how I triage in 2026:

Cut candidates:

  • Overlapping endpoint tooling. If you have EDR and a second "EDR-adjacent" product for historical reasons, consolidate. This is almost always on the table.
  • SIEM log volume for sources you don't actually query. Pull a report of which log sources generated alerts or supported investigations in the last 12 months. The unused ones are costing you $X/GB/month for nothing.
  • "Pilot" tools that never converted to production. Every program has them. Cancel the renewal, don't renegotiate it.
  • Vulnerability scanners you have three of. You do not need three.

Defend at almost any cost:

  • Identity infrastructure — IdP, MFA, privileged access management. This is the attack plane of 2026-27. Do not under-invest.
  • Detection engineering headcount. Tools without people are shelfware. People without tools are still people. Always favor people.
  • Backups, immutability, and recovery testing. The boring stuff that saves the company.
  • Security awareness training that's actually good. Most isn't. The good ones (KnowBe4 done well, Living Security, in-house programs with executive buy-in) earn their keep.

A framework for the conversation

When I walk into a budget review, I bring three documents. No more.

  • A one-page risk register, ranked, with current residual risk and target residual risk per line item. In business terms, not CVE-speak.
  • A one-page ask: what I'm asking for, what it buys, what doesn't get done if it's cut, what the insurance and compliance implications are.
  • A one-page "if you cut me 15%" plan, prepared in advance. Bring it unsolicited. It shows you've done the work, and it controls the narrative of what gets cut if cuts happen.
The security leader who walks in with a pre-drafted cut plan is ten times more credible than the one who defends every line. Credibility compounds. Protect it.

The 2027-specific notes

A few things to weave into this cycle's ask specifically:

  • Identity modernization. Passkey rollout, CAE enablement, phishing-resistant MFA for privileged access. If you have any of this un-finished, 2027 is the year.
  • AI agent security. New line item for most of us. Budget for threat-modeling, eval infrastructure, and monitoring of your own AI deployments. If your organization is spending on AI initiatives, carve a percentage of that for securing them — 8-12% is a defensible number in front of finance.
  • Post-quantum crypto readiness. Not 2027 urgent. But the inventory work — knowing where your long-lived crypto lives — belongs in 2027 so you're not scrambling in 2028. Small ask, large future option value.

The takeaway

The best security budget conversation I ever had ended with the CFO saying "I don't agree with all of this, but you've clearly thought about it harder than I have, so I trust the shape of the ask." That's the bar. You are not trying to win the argument. You are trying to demonstrate that the capital allocation has been thought through by someone who understands the business as well as the threats. Do that, and the number mostly takes care of itself.
